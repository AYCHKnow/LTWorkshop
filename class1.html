Types of Speech Informative

Provides interesting and/or useful information about a topic.
Well researched and organized.

Diversity Within Open Source Software Development

Effects of Communication via Emoji

The Evolution of Michigan Winter in the Age of Global Warming

Types of Speech Informative

Things to think about...

Research and preparation are key to creating a credible and engaging presentation.

Content should be clear and precise. Long, dry lectures tend to bore.

The audience needs to process while you speak, so slow down and include moments of silence.

Success is in the degree to which your audience understands the content.

Types of Speech Demonstrative

Teaches how to do something.
Provides examples and instructions.

How to Theme Your WordPress Blog

Getting Started with Composting

Creating a Company Culture of Empathy

Types of Speech Demonstrative

Things to think about...

Use images, videos, code demos, etc. to reiterate and provide alternate learning cues.

Provide multiple methods for accomplishing goals.

Repeat. Repeat. Repeat.

Success is in the audience member's ability to go home and recall enough information to get started (googling) on their own.

Types of Speech Persuasive

Attempts to persuade the audience to believe something.
Sell, promote, convince.

Change Your Life with the All-Beer Diet

Join the Navy Seals

The 1969 Moon Landing was a Hoax

Types of Speech Persuasive

Things to think about...

Include graphs, data, and supporting documentation to validate your point.

Relate to your audience throughs stories and solutions.

Make it personal. Show enthusiam and emotion.

Success is in the degree to which your audience's beliefs have changed, or in the audience's resolution to act.

Types of Speech Entertaining

Attempts to leave the audience with a favorable impression.
Humor is important.

A Maid Of Honor Speech

Comic Monologues

My Top 10 Favorite Beyonce GIFs

Types of Speech Entertaining

Things to think about...

Provide amusement through humor and storytelling.

Create an emotional connection.

Avoid offending.

Success is in creating an favorable, emotional reaction in the audience.

Planning a Speech

Determine Your Objective

What will you be speaking about?

What do you want the audience to know?

What problem are you trying to solve?

What thing are you trying to change?

What type of talk is most fitting?

Identify Your Audience

Who will you be speaking to?

Why will they be there?

How can you most effectively help them?

What sort of language should you use?

Start to think like your audience.

Back to when you knew nothing...

How did it feel?   What did you struggle with?   How did you learn it?

Name Your Talk

Or, don't.

If you've got a title you like already, go with it!
It'll help steer the direction of your content.

If you're not quite sure about a title yet,
move on to the content and let it steer you to a title.

Outline Your Talk

Do your research.

Determine the most important points and
the order in which it makes sense to present them.

Think about how you'll be able to transition from one point to the next.

Decide how to make a strong first impression in your opening.

Decide how you're closing statements will tie it all together.

5 Steps to Persuasion

  1. Get Their Attention
    Be a little dramatic - arouse interest, drop some crazy stats,
    or a shocking example, or a powerful quote.
  2. Establish A Need
    Demonstrate that a significant problem already exists,
    and that it needs to be fixed.
  3. Provide A Solution
    Propose specific and realistic ways to create satisfaction.
    Preemptively identify and address concerns.
  4. Visualize The Benefits
    Motivate the audience by warning of the consequences of inaction.
    Create urgency, and provide reassurance of your solution.
  5. Invite Action
    Tell them how to get started. Make it easy and immediately doable.
    Provide clear next steps.

Monroe's Motivated Sequence

5 Steps to Persuasion Speaker Perspective

  1. Get Their Attention
    Hey! Listen to me, you have a PROBLEM!
  2. Establish A Need
    Let me EXPLAIN the problem.
  3. Provide A Solution
    But, I have a SOLUTION!
  4. Visualize The Benefits
    If we IMPLEMENT my solution, this is what will happen.
    Or, if we don't implement my solution, this is what will happen.
  5. Invite Action
    You can help me in this specific way. Will you help me?

Wikipedia

5 Steps to Persuasion Audience Reaction

  1. Get Their Attention
    Hey! I want to hear what you have to say!
  2. Establish A Need
    I agree! I totally have that need/want!
  3. Provide A Solution
    I see your solution will work.
  4. Visualize The Benefits
    This is a great idea!
  5. Invite Action
    I want it!

Speech Content

Start simple to hook your audience.

Making them feel live they've already got a basic grasp
of the topic will increase their confidence and ability to absord.

Keep your points focused, directed, and limited.

Too many can cause confusion and be overwhelming.

Prune and simplify your content - make every word count.

Try the Rule of Three.

Speech Content

Repeat yourself.

Keep circling back to the point.

Use analogies and relationships to reinforce the message.

Back it up with facts and data and charts.

Build up the story - don't give it all away at once.

Save your big epiphanies for later dramatic effect.

Tell a story, create dynamic - calm to excited, failure to success, murky to clear.

Tips for Public Speaking

6 I's of Credibility

  • Ideation
    Introduce a new, creative idea to the audience
  • Information
    Present factual information to drives decision making
  • Influence
    Showing confidence increases influence. Be prepared.
  • Integrity
    Be your authentic self on stage. Posers don't generate trust.
  • Impact
    Leave a lasting impression with a memorable message.
  • Ignition
    Serve your purpose by calling people to action.

Owning the Stage & Body Language

Move around in the space you're given.

Standing still or behind a lecturn does not scream confidence.

If you move around, both the floor and within your stance -
you allow your audience to engage with you on a physical level.

Owning the Stage & Body Language

Practice a confident stance.

Stand up straight, feet apart, one foot slightly in front, lean in.

Keep your body open.

Don't clasp your hands or fold your arms.

Relax your arms down or use them to gesture.

Use movement and posture to emphasize your points.

Try it out!

Crutches, Breathing, & Pausing

Eliminate crutch words from your speech.

um, er, uh, like, etc.

These detract from perceived confidence and create tension.

Pause to emphasize points and leave time for thought.

Silence is OK!!

Use it to breathe, relax, sip some water

Face & Voice

Relax your face.

Show emotion. Smile naturally, if it's appropriate. Relax your gaze.

Slow down a little.

Speak at a conversational pace, or a little slower.

Remember to breathe.

Speak with strength and dynamic.

Project your voice, or lower it for dramatics.

Don't speak in monotone.
